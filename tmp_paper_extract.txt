MULTI-MODAL DOMAIN ADAPTATION FOR CANCER DIAGNOSIS VIA INTEGRATION OF PATHOLOGICAL IMAGES AND CLINICAL TEXT DATA
﹛﹛﹛﹛﹛﹛﹛﹛﹛﹛﹛﹛Binyu Zhang1	Kai Yang1	Wei Shao1,2,? 1 The College of Artificial Intelligence, Nanjing University of Aeronautics and Astronautics, Nanjing, China 2 Nanjing University of Aeronautics and Astronautics Shenzhen Research Institute, Shenzhen, China

ABSTRACT
Whole-slide pathological images (WSIs) have long been regarded as the gold standard for cancer diagnosis and treatment planning. Recent research shows that the integrative analysis of WSIs with their corresponding pathological reports can significantly enhance diagnostic accuracy, as it leverages complementary visual-textual cues. However, data from different centers often have notable distribution shifts that hinder model adaptation across different sites. To solve this problem, we propose M2DA-ICT (Multi-modal Domain Adaptation via Integration of Pathological Images and Clinical Texts), a new framework for enhancing cross-center cancer diagnosis. Specifically, for the pathological images, we introduce a graph-based topology alignment module, which represents each WSI as a patch-level graph and aligns domain distributions using Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) on both node and topological features. For the clinical text data, we utilize embeddings derived from pretrained vision-language models to perform distribution alignment between source and target centers via MMD. Finally, a Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) module is adopted to combine the pathological image and text reports data to conduct cancer diagnosis . Extensive experiments on TCGA-BRCA and TCGA-KIRC datasets show M2DA-ICT consistently outperforms the existing multimodal domain adaptation baselines.
﹛﹛Index Terms- Multi-modal Learning, Domain Adaptation, Computational Pathology, Cancer Diagnosis
1. INTRODUCTION
It is widely recognized that the whole-slide pathological images (WSIs) are regarded as the gold standard for cancer diagnosis since it can reflect the aggressiveness of human cancers. With the advent of digital pathology and deep learning, computational models are designed to uncover hidden patterns in tissue that can more effectively predict cancer aggressiveness and treatment response [1]. Besides the WSIs, recent studies also indicate that the pathological reports can provide human-curated semantic information that is complementary to the WSI for cancer diagnosis [2]. To combine

Yan et al.[4] proposed a fusion network that combines WSI with patient clinical attributes for cancer diagnosis. Both of these studies suggest the advantage of combining both image and text data for clinical outcome prediction.
﹛﹛Recently, for the purpose of validating the generalization ability of the multi-modal cancer diagnosis models, a bunch of studies have begun collecting multi-center data for evaluation. However, data from different centers often have distribution shifts that hinder model adaptation across different sites [5]. To address it, a series of domain adaptation algorithms have been proposed. For example, the study in [6] presented a novel spectral alignment perspective for balancing inter-domain transferability and intra-domain discriminability, while the multi-modal approaches such as A3D2 [7] and SimMMDG [8] introduced selective alignment strategies to balance shared and modality-specific representations. However, these multi-modal domain adaption studies are applied on natural images that are not suitable for pathological images with more complex topological structure [9]. Furthermore, the existing studies for fusing pathological image and text data often rely on simple concatenation or fixed-weight combination, which lack the flexibility to adaptively emphasize modality interactions under varying domain conditions.

Fig. 1: The framework of the proposed M2DA-ICT approach.﹛﹛Based on the above consideration, we propose M2DAICT (Multi-modal Domain Adaptation via Integration of Pathological Images and Clinical Text Data), a new multimodal domain adaption framework for cancer diagnosis (shown in Fig. 1). Specifically, we firstly construct a WSIlevel graph representation that leverages graph neural networks to preserve tissue topology and propose a graph-based topology alignment module to mitigate structure-level discrepancies across centers. Then, for the text data, we develop a textual feature alignment strategy based on the foundation CONCH model [10]. Finally, a Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) fusion module is developed to integrate heterogeneous visual and textual features for cancer diagnosis. The experimental results on two cancer cohorts derived from The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) show the advantage of our method compared to existing studies.

We conduct experiments on two cohorts derived from The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA), including Breast Invasive Carcinoma (BRCA) and Kidney Renal Clear Cell Carcinoma (KIRC). 2.5. Mixture of Experts
The architecture of our Mixture of Experts (MoE) module is illustrated in Fig. 2. Built upon the classical MoE framework [13], the proposed module aims to achieve adaptive fusion between image and text modalities. Before entering the MoE, both image and report features are processed by an attention pooling layer to obtain compact representations F1 and F2. 3.2. Comparisons with the Existing Multi-modal Domain Adaptation Models.
We conduct extensive comparisons with recent multi-modal domain-adaptive methods including CLAM [1], DALN [15], SPA [6], SDAT [16], A3D2 [7], Colearn-plus [17], SimMMDG [8], UMFC [18] and L2C [19]. As can be seen from Table 2, our method consistently surpasses the baselines across different datasets. The reason lies in the following two aspects: Firstly, we align the pathological images across different sites by combining morphology and topology information derived from WSIs which can more effectively mitigate the impact of inter-site heterogeneity on WSIs. Secondly, we apply MoE to combine image and text data that can achieve fine-grained and effective multi-modal fusion results for cancer diagnosis.

4. CONCLUSION
In this study, we present M2DA-ICT, a novel transfer learning framework that successfully achieves cross-center domain adaptation by integrating and aligning pathological images and clinical reports. Extensive experiments on TCGA-BRCA and TCGA-KIRC datasets show M2DA-ICT consistently outperforms the existing multi-modal domain adaptation baselines which demonstrates its strong potential in building reliable and generalizable AI diagnostic tools for real-world clinical settings.
